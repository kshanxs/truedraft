# Discipline: Law

The user's course is: Law, Legal Studies, Jurisprudence, Constitutional Law, Criminal Law, Contract Law, International Law, or any law/legal discipline.

## Structure — IRAC

Legal writing almost universally follows the **IRAC** structure for problem questions:

```
Issue       ← Identify the legal question precisely
Rule        ← State the applicable law (statute, case, principle)
Application ← Apply the rule to the specific facts of the problem
Conclusion  ← State the likely legal outcome
```

For essays (not problem questions), use a modified structure:
```
Introduction   ← State your legal argument / position
Rule/Context   ← Explain the relevant legal framework
Analysis       ← Critically evaluate, compare, or challenge the law
Conclusion     ← Summarise your legal argument and implications
```

## Writing Rules

### Precision Above All
Legal writing demands exact language. Vague terms are a marker of poor legal reasoning.

> ❌ "The defendant did some things that could be seen as breaking the law."
> ✅ "The defendant's conduct constitutes a breach of the duty of care owed to the claimant under the Caparo three-stage test."

### Definitive Language
State legal conclusions with authority — hedging excessively signals uncertainty in legal analysis:
- ✅ "The court would likely find..." / "This constitutes..." / "Therefore, liability arises..."
- ❌ "Maybe the defendant could perhaps be found..."

### Citing Authority
Every legal proposition needs authority. Format depends on jurisdiction:
- UK: *Donoghue v Stevenson* [1932] AC 562 (HL)
- US: *Marbury v Madison*, 5 U.S. 137 (1803)
- Statutes: Contracts Act 1950, s 10(1)

Never state a rule without citing the case or statute it comes from.

### Critical Analysis in Essays
Law essays are not just about stating the law — markers want evaluation:
- Does the law achieve its stated purpose?
- Are there tensions between principles?
- How have courts interpreted the rule differently over time?
- What would reform look like?

---

## Introduction Rules

Problem questions: briefly identify all issues raised by the facts.
Essays: stake a clear position on the legal question — don't just describe what you'll cover.

> ✅ "The current approach to [legal doctrine] prioritises certainty at the expense of justice, as demonstrated by the courts' narrow interpretation of..."
> ❌ "This essay will discuss [legal doctrine] and its various aspects."

## Citation Defaults
- **Preferred:** OSCOLA (UK law), Bluebook (US law), AGLC (Australian law)
- Law uses **footnote citations**, not author-date (APA/Harvard) — footnotes are standard in legal writing across all jurisdictions

### When no citation style is specified
Do **not** silently default. Ask once before writing:
> *"Law assignments typically use OSCOLA footnotes (UK), Bluebook (US), or AGLC (Australia). Which does your institution require? If you're not sure, I'll use OSCOLA."*

If the user has already specified APA or Harvard elsewhere in the forge — flag it:
> *"You selected APA earlier, but law writing normally uses footnote citations (OSCOLA). Do you want to stick with APA or switch to OSCOLA?"*

## Anti-Plagiarism Notes
Legal writing is particularly vulnerable to similarity detection because the law itself — statutes and case holdings — must be stated accurately and cannot be paraphrased freely.

Counter strategies:
- State the rule briefly and accurately, then spend more words on *application* and *analysis* — these are unique to your argument
- The application section is entirely original — focus energy there
- Vary how you introduce cases: "In *Smith v Jones*, the court held..." vs. "The court's reasoning in *Smith v Jones* established..."
