# Discipline: Humanities

## Load when
The user's course is: Literature, History, Philosophy, Cultural Studies, Film Studies, Art History, Linguistics, Religious Studies, or any humanities field.

---

## Structure — Argument First

Humanities writing is **argument-driven**, not topic-driven. The structure flows from your central claim:

```
Introduction     ← State your argument (thesis) clearly upfront
Body Paragraphs  ← Each paragraph advances one aspect of the argument
                   supported by textual evidence + your interpretation
Counterargument  ← Acknowledge the strongest opposing view, then rebut it
Conclusion       ← Restate how the evidence supports your argument;
                   open a broader question
```

Every paragraph should have a **topic sentence** that makes a mini-argument, not just a topic statement.

> ❌ "This paragraph will discuss the role of light imagery in the novel."
> ✅ "The recurring imagery of artificial light in the novel functions less as comfort than as surveillance — a visual language of control masquerading as care."

---

## Writing Rules

### Thesis
The thesis must be arguable — not a fact, not a summary. A reader should be able to disagree with it.

> ❌ "Shakespeare's Hamlet deals with themes of grief and revenge."
> ✅ "Hamlet's delay is not weakness but a deliberate refusal to participate in the cycle of patriarchal violence that destroyed his father."

### Textual Evidence
- Quote from primary sources directly and briefly
- Never let a quote stand alone — always follow with interpretation
- Format: quote → what it shows → why it matters to your argument

### Interpretation over Description
Humanities markers penalise plot summary and description.

> ❌ "In Chapter 3, the protagonist walks through the ruins of the city."
> ✅ "The protagonist's movement through the ruins enacts the novel's central tension between historical witness and personal survival — she cannot look away, but she also cannot fully see."

### Hedging vs. Confidence
Humanities writing uses *interpretive confidence*, not scientific hedging:
- ✅ "The text suggests..." / "One reading of this is..." / "This implies..."
- ❌ "It is possible that perhaps the author may have intended..."

---

## Introduction Rules

Open by situating the argument within a critical conversation or broader tension:
> ✅ "How a society represents its undesirable histories reveals as much about its present anxieties as its past events. In [text], the treatment of colonial memory..."
> ❌ "This essay will discuss colonial memory in [text]."

---

## Citation Defaults
- **Preferred:** MLA 9 (literature), Chicago (history, philosophy)
- Cite page numbers for quotes; cite secondary sources for theoretical frameworks

---

## Anti-Plagiarism Notes
In humanities, close-reading passages are the highest risk — the same quotes get cited by many students.

Counter strategies:
- Choose less-cited passages that support your argument equally well
- Lead with your interpretation before quoting, not after
- Connect the quote to your specific thesis rather than general theme
