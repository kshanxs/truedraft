# Humanise Reference

## Purpose

Transform stiff, over-academic, or AI-sounding writing into prose that reads naturally — like it was written by a thoughtful person, not generated by a machine. This reference is for readability: clear, warm, flowing language that still sounds intelligent and credible.

> **Distinct from `style_randomization.md`**, which targets AI detection scores. This file targets *human readability* — making the writing genuinely enjoyable and effortless to read.

---

## Tone Modes

Humanise has two settings. Infer from context or ask the user:

| Mode | When to use | Feel |
|---|---|---|
| **Academic-Conversational** | Essays, reports, formal assignments | Sounds like a smart person talking, not a textbook |
| **Fully Conversational** | Explainers, reflective pieces, blog-style work | Natural, warm, clear — like a knowledgeable friend explaining something |

Default to **Academic-Conversational** unless the user specifies otherwise or the tone is explicitly informal.

---

## Core Rules

### 1. Prefer Active Voice
Passive voice creates distance. Swap it where it sounds cold:
> ❌ "It was found that the results were inconsistent across trials."
> ✅ "The results varied across trials — a pattern that suggests the approach needs refinement."

Exceptions: Keep passive when the subject is genuinely unknown or irrelevant (science methods sections).

### 2. Cut Filler Phrases
Strip phrases that add length but not meaning:
| ❌ Remove | ✅ Replace with |
|---|---|
| "It is important to note that..." | Just say the thing. |
| "In order to..." | "To..." |
| "Due to the fact that..." | "Because..." |
| "At this point in time..." | "Now..." |
| "It can be argued that..." | Make the argument. |
| "There is a significant amount of..." | "Much of..." / give a number |

### 3. Use Concrete, Specific Language
Abstract language loses readers. Ground every idea in something tangible:
> ❌ "The implementation of various technological solutions has resulted in improved operational efficiency."
> ✅ "Using automated inventory tracking cut stock-check time from two hours to fifteen minutes."

### 4. Open Sentences with the Main Idea
Don't bury the point in subordinate clauses:
> ❌ "Given the challenges posed by the ongoing shifts in consumer behaviour and the increasing volatility of global supply chains, firms have had to adapt."
> ✅ "Firms are adapting fast — and they have to, given volatile supply chains and shifting consumer habits."

### 5. Use Analogies for Abstract Concepts
One good analogy is worth three explanatory sentences:
> ❌ "Overfitting occurs when a model learns the training data too specifically to generalise to new data."
> ✅ "Overfitting is like a student who memorises exam answers verbatim — they ace the practice paper, then fail when the questions are rephrased."

Use analogies for genuinely complex ideas, not for simple ones (that makes writing condescending).

### 6. Vary Sentence Rhythm — With Intent
Short sentences land hard. Use them after long ones to create emphasis:
> "The algorithm was simple in design, lightweight in memory usage, and remarkably robust across diverse test conditions. It worked."

The final two-word sentence carries more punch *because* of what came before it.

### 7. Use Contractions Sparingly (Academic-Conversational Only)
One or two contractions per page signals a human voice without undermining formality:
- "It's worth noting..." / "This doesn't mean..." / "That's not the whole story."
- Avoid in fully formal submissions unless the assignment explicitly permits informal tone.

### 8. Avoid Overused Academic Openers
These are hallmarks of AI-generated or overly formal writing — replace them:
| ❌ Overused | ✅ Alternative |
|---|---|
| "In today's rapidly changing world..." | Start with a specific claim or observation |
| "Throughout history, mankind has..." | "For centuries, people have..." or just make the point |
| "This essay will discuss..." | State your argument instead |
| "Firstly... Secondly... Thirdly..." | Embed transitions naturally |
| "Furthermore, moreover, additionally..." | Use "Also", "And", or restructure |

### 9. Let Punctuation Do Work
Dashes, colons, and semicolons create rhythm and control pace:
> "The solution works — but only if the data is clean."
> "Three things drive customer loyalty: reliability, value, and trust."
> "The model performed well on the validation set; the test results told a different story."

### 10. End Sections with a Human Thought
Avoid conclusions that just restate the paragraph. End with a small insight, implication, or forward-looking thought:
> ❌ "In summary, machine learning requires large amounts of training data."
> ✅ "In the end, the quality of a machine learning model is only ever as good as the data it was fed — which makes data curation as important as any algorithm choice."

---

## What NOT to Change

- Do not remove evidence, citations, or factual claims
- Do not change technical terminology that must remain precise
- Do not make the writing so casual it loses academic credibility
- Do not alter the argument, structure, or conclusion

---

## Humanise Intensity Scale

If the user doesn't specify, default to **Level 2**.

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| 1 — Light touch | Remove filler phrases, activate key sentences, minor rhythm fixes |
| 2 — Standard | Full rules above applied; one or two analogies added where helpful |
| 3 — Conversational | Contractions permitted; more analogies; short punchy sentences used freely; warmer tone throughout |

---

## Output Format

After humanising, optionally show a **Before / After** comparison for 2–3 of the most significantly changed sentences, so the user can see what changed and why.
