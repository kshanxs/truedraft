# Academic Skill Framework

## Purpose

This file defines the core writing rules used by the Writer Agent. It governs how concepts are introduced, explained, and structured to maximize originality and minimize similarity to common academic sources.

---

## Section 1 — Core Writing Philosophy

1. Avoid reproducing sentences from textbooks, research articles, or common web sources
2. Explain concepts through reasoning, not by copying definitions
3. Prioritize explanation and interpretation over restating existing descriptions
4. Maintain academic tone with original sentence construction
5. Combine ideas from multiple angles rather than summarizing one source

---

## Section 2 — Introduction Writing Rule (Mandatory)

The introduction must **never** begin with a definition-style opening.

**Prohibited openings:**
- "X is defined as..."
- "In today's world..."
- "X plays an important role in..."
- "This essay will discuss..."
- "According to [author], X is..."

**Choose one of these analytical approaches instead:**

### Analytical Context
Start with an observation about the field or phenomenon.
> "The rapid expansion of digital information systems has transformed how modern organizations process, store, and act on data."

### Problem Framing
Introduce a challenge related to the topic.
> "As datasets grow in volume and complexity, extracting meaningful patterns without introducing bias becomes one of the central challenges in computational research."

### Historical Perspective
Describe how the topic has evolved.
> "Over the past two decades, advances in processing power combined with new algorithmic techniques have fundamentally reshaped how intelligent systems are designed."

**Pro tip**: Introductions and definitions are the highest-risk sections for plagiarism detection — they're where everyone writes the same thing. A contextual introduction alone can reduce similarity by 10–15%.

---

## Section 3 — Structural Originality

Assignments should follow this reasoning-oriented structure:

1. Analytical introduction
2. Concept explanation (using original reasoning)
3. Real-world example or application
4. Discussion or comparative analysis
5. Critical insight or observation
6. Conclusion with a perspective

Avoid copying the typical textbook flow: definition → theory → application → summary.

---

## Section 4 — Semantic Expansion Strategy

For each concept, apply the **three-step expansion rule**:

1. **Explain** the concept using your own reasoning
2. **Provide** a short concrete example or scenario
3. **Discuss** the implications or significance

This increases semantic distance from source material because the same idea is expressed in three different registers.

**Example:**

Instead of: *"Overfitting occurs when a model performs well on training data but poorly on new data."*

Write:
> "A model that has been tuned too closely to its training examples often struggles to adapt to new inputs — a condition known as overfitting. Consider a student who memorizes exam answers without understanding the underlying concepts: they succeed on familiar questions but fail when the phrasing changes. In practice, overfitting is controlled through techniques like regularization and cross-validation, which help ensure a model's performance generalizes beyond its training set."

---

## Section 5 — N-Gram Similarity Prevention

Avoid repeating sequences of 3–5 consecutive words that commonly appear in academic literature.

**Methods:**
- Change sentence word order
- Convert passive voice to active voice (or vice versa) for variety
- Replace common academic phrases with conceptual alternatives
- Break long sentences into two focused statements

---

## Section 6 — Citation Policy

When referencing established theories or research:
- Cite the author rather than paraphrasing their exact wording
- Prefer citing ideas over copying sentence structure

**Format example:**
> "Deep learning has demonstrated remarkable performance in image classification tasks (LeCun et al., 1998)."

---

## Section 7 — Final Quality Rule

Before handing off to the Style Agent, confirm:

- [ ] Introduction does not start with a definition
- [ ] Every concept includes an example
- [ ] No sequence of 4+ words matches common textbook phrasing
- [ ] Academic tone is maintained throughout
