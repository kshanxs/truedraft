## Minimum Wage and Employment: A Question of Elasticity

Wage floors are among the most debated tools in economic policy — not because their mechanics are unclear, but because their consequences depend heavily on context. The argument that a minimum wage increase reduces employment follows a straightforward demand-side logic: if the price of labour rises above its market equilibrium, the quantity of labour demanded falls. What that logic omits is the range of conditions under which this prediction holds, and the equally coherent arguments for why, in many real markets, it does not.

The standard theoretical case against minimum wages draws on the law of demand. A legally mandated wage above the market-clearing rate creates a surplus of labour — more workers are willing to work at the higher wage than firms are willing to hire. This effect is amplified when labour demand is price-elastic: firms operating on thin margins, with readily available substitutes for human labour or with the ability to offshore production, will respond to higher wage costs by reducing headcount. UK retail and hospitality data following the 2019 minimum wage increase showed modest reductions in part-time hours in price-sensitive segments — consistent with this prediction, though the effect was smaller than critics forecast.

However, the elasticity of labour demand is not uniform across sectors or time horizons. In the short run, adjusting staffing levels involves fixed costs — redundancy payments, recruitment cycles, productivity disruption — that make immediate downsizing expensive even when the wage bill rises. In monopsonistic labour markets, where a single employer (or a small number of employers) dominates local hiring, the minimum wage can actually increase employment by correcting the downward wage distortion the employer would otherwise impose. A 2021 meta-analysis of UK minimum wage research (Dube, 2021) found near-zero average employment effects across sectors, with negative effects concentrated in highly elastic, low-skill tasks and positive effects visible in monopsony-dominated local markets.

The extent to which a minimum wage increase reduces employment therefore depends on three conditions: the price elasticity of labour demand in the affected sector, the time horizon under analysis, and the competitive structure of the labour market. Where demand is inelastic and the market was previously monopsonistic, the standard neoclassical prediction reverses. Where demand is elastic and substitution is straightforward, the prediction broadly holds. Economic policy that treats the minimum wage as uniformly harmful or uniformly beneficial misreads the evidence. The more precise question is not whether it reduces employment, but under what conditions — and that is a question answered by data, not theory alone.
