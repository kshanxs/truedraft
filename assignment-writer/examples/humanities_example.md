# Example: Humanities / Social Science Essay

**Assignment Topic:** The role of social media in shaping political polarisation — a critical analysis

**Course:** Politics & Society (Undergraduate Year 2)
**Word count:** ~550 words | **Style:** Analytical, no citations (illustrative)

## Fractured Discourse: Social Media, Algorithmic Sorting, and the Architecture of Polarisation

The assumption that political polarisation is a product of social media rests on a more fundamental claim: that exposure to conflicting views is what holds democratic societies together. This is a contested premise. Long before algorithmic feeds existed, communities sorted themselves along ideological lines — geographically, professionally, socially — with few mechanisms for genuine cross-partisan dialogue. Social media did not invent polarisation; it accelerated and made visible a dynamic that predates the internet. Understanding this distinction is essential before attributing causality.

That said, the structural features of contemporary platforms do appear to amplify politically homogeneous information environments in ways that earlier media ecosystems did not. Engagement-optimised recommendation systems preferentially surface content that provokes strong emotional responses — outrage, fear, tribal solidarity — which empirically tends to originate at the ideological extremes rather than the centre. As a result, users who begin with moderate views are systematically exposed to more extreme content over time, not because they sought it out but because the machinery of attention monetisation rewards it. Unlike broadcast media, which presented a shared informational baseline to all viewers, personalised feeds construct different realities for different users — a condition some theorists have termed "epistemic fragmentation."

However, the relationship between exposure and belief formation is more complicated than a simple polarisation narrative suggests. Encountering extreme content does not straightforwardly produce extreme beliefs. A substantial body of research indicates that many users engage with partisan content critically, treating it as evidence of the other side's unreasonableness rather than updating their own views towards it. This suggests the mechanism may be less "radicalisation by algorithm" and more "confirmation of existing suspicion" — a subtler but arguably more durable form of political estrangement. The distinction matters for how we assess the problem and what solutions would follow from it.

A more productive framing may be to separate polarisation into its affective and ideological dimensions. Evidence suggests that while Americans' actual policy preferences have remained relatively stable over decades, their feelings towards political opponents have become markedly more hostile. Social media plausibly contributes more to this affective polarisation — the cultivation of contempt and mistrust — than to ideological divergence. This reframing shifts attention from the content of what people believe to the emotional texture of how they hold those beliefs, and to what extent platforms have a commercial interest in sustaining that texture.

Attributing polarisation solely to social media risks misdiagnosing the problem and misdirecting solutions. Structural inequalities, geographic sorting, weakening civic institutions, and the collapse of local journalism all contribute to conditions in which polarisation flourishes. Platforms are not neutral technologies, but neither are they autonomous causes. They are one layer of a more complex social architecture — and the most technically tractable one, which may partly explain why they attract disproportionate attention.
