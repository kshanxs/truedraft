## Draft Excerpt

Remote work does not produce a single productivity outcome across all organizations. Its effect depends on the type of work being measured, the degree of employee autonomy, and the quality of coordination systems that sit behind day-to-day tasks. For individual contributors whose work is evaluated through discrete outputs, remote arrangements may remove commuting friction and increase usable focus time. For coordination-heavy teams, however, those gains can be offset when weak documentation or unclear decision ownership slows execution. `[CITE: external productivity study]`

This is why hybrid structures often appear more durable than a full return-to-office or a fully remote default. They preserve some of the autonomy associated with remote work while giving teams predictable moments for handoff, review, and informal problem-solving. The arrangement is not automatically better, but it is often easier to govern because managers can design around known collaboration points rather than hoping they emerge spontaneously. `[CITE: internal survey results]`
