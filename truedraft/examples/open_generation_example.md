## Draft Excerpt

Coastal adaptation planning often fails not because cities misunderstand physical risk, but because they isolate that risk from the institutions and households expected to absorb it. Sea walls, drainage upgrades, and zoning restrictions matter, yet their effect depends on who can pay for them, who is displaced by them, and which neighborhoods are treated as worth protecting first. That turns adaptation into a governance problem as much as an engineering one.

Infrastructure illustrates the point. A drainage upgrade may reduce flood exposure in one district while shifting pressure into another if surrounding land use remains unchanged. The policy looks technically competent on paper, but its real performance depends on the wider urban system.
