# Academic Rules

## Purpose

Core drafting rules for `TrueDraft`. These rules push the writing toward analysis and specificity rather than boilerplate phrasing.

---

## Core Writing Philosophy

1. Explain ideas through reasoning, not copied definitions
2. Prefer a clear argument over generic filler
3. Use examples sparingly and avoid default textbook examples when a fresher one fits
4. Let the user's notes steer the draft whenever they exist
5. Mark unsupported claims instead of hiding them

---

## Introduction Rule

The introduction must not begin with a dictionary-style definition.

**Prohibited openings:**

- "X is defined as..."
- "In today's world..."
- "X plays an important role in..."
- "This essay will discuss..."
- "According to [author], X is..." as the opening move

Use one of these opening moves instead:

- analytical context
- problem framing
- historical perspective

---

## Default Structure

1. Analytical introduction
2. Concept explanation
3. Real-world example or application
4. Discussion, comparison, or limitation
5. Critical insight or observation
6. Conclusion with a perspective

Avoid sounding like a chapter summary.

---

## Paragraph Rule

For each major concept:

1. Explain it
2. Give a concrete example
3. State why it matters

If an example is too clichéd for the topic, choose another one.

---

## Source Honesty

If a claim needs a source and none is provided:

- mark it with `[CITE: ...]` or `[UNVERIFIED: ...]`
- do not fabricate an author or source
- do not generate a final bibliography entry from imagination
